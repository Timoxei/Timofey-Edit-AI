The Nate Friedman Show — Full Script with Research

1. Cold Open / Hook

NATE (walking through NYC, direct to camera):

Two hundred and seventy-six thousand dollars. That's the median wealth of a white household in New York City.

Eighteen thousand dollars. That's the median wealth of a black household.

New York's new mayor, Zohran Mamdani, says he's going to fix that. With a 375-page plan. 45 agencies. 800 strategies. And a whole lot of people calling it the White People Tax.

So I went to the streets of New York to ask people what they think.

2. Welcome + What's in the Plan

NATE:

Welcome back to the Nate Friedman Show.

Before we get to the interviews, let me break down what this plan actually says. Because it's 375 pages, and most people haven't read it.

It's called the Preliminary Citywide Racial Equity Plan — the first government-wide racial equity framework in New York City history. It covers everything from policing to healthcare to housing to taxes.

Here's what stands out:

  • It says New York's history is rooted in "settler colonialism, exploitation, and racial oppression." That's a direct quote.
  • It wants the NYPD executive ranks to reflect the diverse population of New York City. Since white people are a minority of the city, that means fewer white people in leadership.
  • It wants to "uproot white supremacy" in the health department.
  • It proposes shifting the tax burden from outer boroughs to "richer and Whiter neighborhoods." That's also a direct quote.
  • The Office of Racial Equity gets $10.2 million — a 42% budget increase — while the city faces a $5.4 to $7.1 billion shortfall.

And 62% of all New Yorkers — not just Black or Hispanic — fall below the city's own cost-of-living threshold. A family of four needs $160,000 a year just to get by here.

So I went out and asked the question directly: if Mamdani said he needed to raise white people's taxes by 2% to close the wealth gap, would you support that?

3. Interview Block 1 — First Reactions

Interview A: Colombian Immigrants (Two Men)

NATE: What do you guys think of Donny so far, his first 100 days?

MAN 1: I didn't vote for him.

NATE: So he just released a racial equity plan. It's a 375-page document. One of the things it explicitly states is that the average white household income is $276,000 and the average black income is $18,000. And they're looking to switch resources around to fix that. What do you think that could entail?

MAN 1: I'm a Latino, so yeah, I hope to work, you know? I honestly don't think it will happen.

NATE: In the document it talks about hiring people based off of race. The NYPD — they want to change the executive level to reflect the demographics. If you have a white candidate who is more qualified than a black candidate, do you think the inequity should be resolved by hiring the black person?

MAN 1: I don't really care about the color. When somebody is more qualified... That's the reason I want to tell you — we're from Colombia. When we get new governments, they come and they bring their own people, and we have engineers running hospitals who have no idea what they're doing. They got the job because they're connected to the government.

"We have engineers running hospitals who have no idea what they're doing. They got the job because they're connected to the government."

MAN 1: I have a friend who runs a hospital, Northwell, and they're having a big issue because they tell them they have to hire some specific type of group of people. And he said sometimes they're like, I don't really think that's the most qualified, but you have to.

NATE: One of the things it mentions about the health department is that they want to uproot white supremacy. Do you feel that there's white supremacy in New York?

MAN 1: Absolutely not. I've been here for 30 years, and I've never felt like I've been discriminated against. I don't get a bigger job because I didn't go to a great school. I don't have a big college degree. But I'm sure if I had the opportunity and I happened to be very smart, I would have got the job.

Public Housing Deep-Cut (Same Interview)

NATE: The plan says New York City public housing is 90% Black or Hispanic, with a median household income of $17,000, and Congress has created an $80 billion capital shortfall. Would you support raising your taxes to fund that gap?

MAN 1: Absolutely not. And I'll tell you why. I'm a runner. And there's nothing I hate more — when I go through the projects at 5:30 in the morning and I see people healthier and taller than me smoking weed at 5:30 in the morning. That should be for people who really need help.

"I see people healthier than me smoking weed at 5:30 in the morning. That should be for people who really need help."

MAN 1: This is sort of like a system, like a scam — people just stayed there for generations, and I have to take half of my taxes to support that? If you tell me there's an older lady who just lost her husband with three kids — absolutely. But there's some sort of scandal that I don't support.

NATE: This whole plan is saying it's to help nonwhite people. So your response to this concept?

MAN 1: I'm an immigrant. 30 years here. I never took any help from the government. I've been working since I moved here. White people born and raised here — to get help? Why? They can find a job the way that I did.

I'm a Democrat, but there's something that I hate about this. Everything has to be free. Free education. Free. Free. Free. I'm sick and tired — everything has to be free.

5. The Numbers

NATE:

Let me give you the numbers, because the numbers are what make this interesting.

The wealth gap is real: $276,000 median for white households versus $18,000 for Black households. That's a 15-to-1 ratio.

But here's what the plan doesn't tell you:

  • 62% of all New Yorkers fall below the city's own True Cost of Living threshold. Not just Black New Yorkers — everyone.
  • 44% of white New Yorkers are below that threshold. 66% of Black. 78% of Hispanic. 63% of Asian.
  • A family of four needs nearly $160,000 a year just to survive in this city. The federal welfare line is under $80,000.
  • The city's budget shortfall is $5.4 to $7.1 billion. The Office of Racial Equity gets $10.2 million — that's a 42% increase. Where's the rest coming from?

So the question isn't really about race. The question is: can New York afford any of this?

7. Interview Block 3 — The Other Side

NATE:

Now, not everyone was against it. I talked to people who genuinely believe in the plan. And what's interesting is even the supporters had limits.

Interview G: NYC Native — The Supporter

NATE: If Mamdani needed to raise white people's taxes by 2% to close the wealth gap, would you support that?

MAN: Yes. Because I feel like white people benefit more than we do. When it comes to the pay rate and all that stuff.

NATE: How do white people benefit?

MAN: It's more based on opportunities. Options. I can speak from experience.

NATE: A lot of right-wingers say what you're talking about is racism — racism against white people. What would be your response?

MAN: No, it's not racist. I just feel like everybody should be equal. The difference is definitely obvious.

NATE (to camera): Kind of interesting. He obviously sees that I'm white and feels confident, feels safe saying "tax white people more." So... okay.

Interview H: Kazakh Single Mom — The Contradiction

NATE: What are your thoughts on Mamdani?

WOMAN: Oh, I love him. He's down to earth.

NATE: What do you feel about his socialist ideas — freezing the rent, raising the minimum wage?

WOMAN: I like to help people.

NATE: You're from Kazakhstan. Former Soviet Union. Socialism and communism didn't go too well there. What makes you think it'll go differently?

WOMAN: The communism was like a religion. But I think Mamdani — he uses socialism, but with the feeling of freedom.

NATE: If Mamdani said he needed to raise white people's taxes by 2% to close the wealth gap, would you support that?

WOMAN: I don't think so. I'm a single mom with two kids. It's not fair.

NATE: He wants the NYPD to have less white police officers. How do you feel?

WOMAN: In New York City, you see officers of different types of color. I don't think we need this.

NATE: What about public housing — do you want to fund it?

WOMAN: Yes. It's a good idea. Sometimes people don't have money.

NATE: People spend decades in public housing. Do you think there should be a limit?

10. DOJ Steps In

NATE:

So the people have spoken. But it's not just New Yorkers who have opinions on this plan.

The Department of Justice is now reviewing the Racial Equity Plan. Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon called it "fishy" and said her office is looking into whether it violates existing civil rights law.

Think about that. A plan designed to promote racial equity might itself be illegal racial discrimination.

The Washington Examiner called it "a plan to inflict pain on New York." The Steve Gruber Show called it an "anti-white 375-page equity plan." And the Mamdani administration? They say it's open for public comment for the next 30 days before the final version drops.

11. Close

NATE (walking, sunset NYC skyline behind):

I talked to immigrants from Colombia, Kazakhstan, Russia, Israel. I talked to Democrats, people who hate Trump, people who love Mamdani. I talked to people from LA, Texas, Queens, Brooklyn.

And the one thing almost everyone agreed on? Merit should come first.

The wealth gap is real. $276,000 versus $18,000 — that's not something you can ignore. But the solution? That's what divides people.

A 375-page plan that talks about "settler colonialism" and "uprooting white supremacy" in the health department — that didn't land with the Latinos who've been here 30 years, the Jewish voters worried about antisemitism, or the single mom from Kazakhstan who just wants to work.

The prediction markets give the big promises — taxing billionaires, $30 minimum wage — a 10 to 12% chance. The rent freeze? 79%. The money is telling you something.

The plan is open for public comment for 30 days. Whether it survives the DOJ is another question entirely.
