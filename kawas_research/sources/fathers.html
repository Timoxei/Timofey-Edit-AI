    Disappearing Fathers: A Reflection on Fathers Day & Ramadan.

    Disappearing father

    Waiting

    Standing on linoleum floors

    Old gum stuck in the tracks

    Metal detectors ringing

    Living life in and out of court rooms

    Growing up to see your father in shackles

    It’s been a decade since I’ve been able to celebrate father’s day with my Baba. We’ve been apart for so long now, that it’s become hard to imagine what  life was like with him in the first place.

    My father was incarcerated by the Immigration & Customs Enforcement officers when I was in 7th grade. Since then, after several years in jail and his eventual deportation to Jordan; we have lived a decade apart. In those ten years my father has missed 4 high school graduations, 3 college graduations, one wedding, and the birth of two granddaughters that he still has not met. The older I grow the more and more I realize how much this time period affected everything in my life and still affects it until today.

    Sacrifice. 

    Growing up my we didn’t know my father really well as children. He was always out, always working. We have memories of trips and good times together, but we were young, playing with toys, and he was trying to make a living. We didn’t realize the worth of cementing relationships back then.


    Ya Baba, how would you have known that you would have never seen us grow into adults?

    My father  missed me  go through my most formative years, develop my identity, accomplish my goals- as much as he wanted to, he wasn’t able to be there. There is such a gaping hole that you feel in your life growing up without a father to cheer you on, to  help you make vital decisions,  and growing up with a mother who always had two play two roles .

    incarceration nation

    In those following years that we visited my father while he was incarcerated, my siblings and I watched hundreds of fathers like my own struggling to build relationships with their children. They spoke behind glass windows, across distant tables, in orange jumpsuits and handcuffs. Fatherhood was interrupted, advice about school and friends had to be put on hold. They were too busy trying to cram in hellos, updates, and legal advice in the 45 minutes allotted for “ family time”. Too consumed trying to figure out how to get out, how not to get sent away.

    Arrested for their identities.

    Behind bars that didn’t care.

    memories

    I remember one year during Father’s day, in one of the many jails we visited my dad at, we were allowed to take a picture together. My father was so excited that day because he knew he could keep it with him while we were apart. I still remember feeling so angry, that this was his glorious moment -snapping a polaroid in a beige jumpsuit with half his family. My brother was sitting outside in the waiting room, because we all weren’t allowed in at the same time.

    Security systems in between us,

    These jail systems - I’ll never forgive them.

    One year, we didn’t remember to send a card for Father’s Day.

    On our birthdays he used to send us extravagant cards made of magazine clippings he spent hours and days collaging together. The next week for my sister’s birthday he sent a letter with her card asking us if we had forgotten about him. In the hustle and bustle of trying to live life above water, and in between all the stress, we didn’t realize. We didn’t realize that all he had for those lonely hours were the cards we sent.

    How could have we forgotten?

    Guilt.

    We didn’t  know  how much he loved us until we were too far away from each other to know that love.

    Ramadan

    This father’s day touches me especially as it takes place in the month of Ramadan.  As I go through the Quran this year I pass by so many beautiful stories of fathers and their children. 

    Surat al Baqara : The bond of Ibrahim and Ismail as they built the first house of Allah (swt) together.

    Surat Yusef: The longing Yaqub felt for the son he thought was forever lost to him.

    We cried the tears of Yacub for the fathers we lost. 

     Reading these stories can sometimes be painful as I long for those relationships highlighted in the Quran. I never got the chance to build those special bonds with my dad. For hundreds of Muslim families in the post 9/11 period these relationships were impossible to be built as we were separated by the state through a ruthless immigration system that shatters families.

    Like glass that can never be put back together again.

    These ayat remind me how in Islam there is an emphasis on fatherhood, on family, on lineage on legacy. However for many Muslims, as fatherhood was disappeared from our families- we understand these things differently.

    Our families were split apart, never to be whole again.

    Our lineage is pain, it’s humiliation, it’s detention , it’s separation.

    Our legacy is our fight, that we will never accept this state, or these states that break us apart based on borders drawn on a paper, and walls constructed to blockade us.

    Our families are part of the growing number of families in this country and around the world that have been split apart through migration and death- state law and state violence.

    Immigration, national security, criminal (in) justice- all systems that were meant to break us.

    This Ramadan as you  pray for this ummah, pray for the fathers who are no longer with their families due to the policies of this country. Pray for the mothers who are sent away, or are locked away behind bars. Pray for the children who suffer from the gaping holes in their lives. Moreover, pray for the hundreds of thousands of people, stateless, migrating, escaping, arrested, entrapped, and toiling away. Those who left their homes and lands looking for ways out of the destruction they come from, destruction that was brought upon them, by the very states who push them away.

    Days we spend apart
    Hours we dream of going back
    Lives we lost
    Living only in our imagination
    To go back home
    To go back home
    To go back home
    All we dream of
    Lost memories that have shattered


    Today

    As for now, I haven’t seen my father’s face in two years. Sometimes I forget again, am too busy to remember to talk on skype. Sometimes I just don’t know what to say.

    This Father’s Day we’ll be lucky enough to celebrate over the static of internet calls.

    7 hrs apart in time

    9000  miles apart in distance

    13 hours apart  flying time.

    10 years lost.
